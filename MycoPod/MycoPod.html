The 2025 Eaton Fire

In January 2025, the Eaton Fire swept through Altadena, California — a historic, densely populated community in the foothills of the San Gabriel Mountains. The fire destroyed hundreds of homes and released toxic ash that settled into residential soils.

Among the most concerning consequences was contamination by heavy metals — particularly lead, commonly used in paint and plumbing in older homes throughout the area — as well as polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) from combustion of organic materials.

Why Lead Contamination Matters

Lead is a potent neurotoxin with no safe level of exposure. It is particularly dangerous for children, causing irreversible neurological damage and developmental delays. The California screening level is 80 ppm; the EPA screening level is 200 ppm; hazardous waste begins near 1,000 ppm.

Traditional remediation — soil excavation and disposal — is expensive, disruptive, and simply relocates contamination to landfills. Less invasive, in-situ approaches are urgently needed.

What is Mycoremediation?

Mycoremediation is the use of fungi to degrade or sequester environmental contaminants. Fungi interact with metals through biosorption (passive binding to cell wall chitin), bioaccumulation (active intracellular uptake), and bioprecipitation (converting dissolved ions into insoluble forms).

The mycelium — the vast thread-like root network — creates enormous surface area that interacts with soil particles and pore water, making it a uniquely effective biological sorbent.

Why Oyster Mushrooms?

Pleurotus ostreatus (oyster mushrooms) were chosen because they are among the most extensively studied species in mycoremediation research, with documented heavy metal bioaccumulation. They are commercially available as grain spawn, relatively fast-growing, hardy in outdoor conditions, and non-pathogenic.

While this first iteration focuses on oyster mushrooms, future iterations may test additional species with different metal-binding profiles.

Experimental Design

The Science of MycoPod

A proof-of-concept investigation into whether Pleurotus ostreatus can sequester lead from Eaton Fire–contaminated soil under real outdoor conditions.

Research Question

Can Pleurotus ostreatus (oyster mushrooms) sequester lead from soil contaminated by wildfire ash under outdoor field conditions?

Hypothesis

If oyster mushroom mycelium is inoculated into lead-contaminated soil from the Eaton Fire site, the post-experiment ICP-MS analysis will show a measurable reduction in soil lead concentration compared to the baseline, indicating sequestration of lead into fungal biomass.

Experimental Workflow

01

Soil Collection

Collect contaminated soil from Eaton Fire–affected site in Altadena, CA. Homogenize on a tarp for uniform lead distribution.

02

Baseline ICP-MS

Submit soil aliquots to Professor Tissot's Caltech lab for parts-per-million lead concentration measurement before any inoculation.

03

Inoculation

Layer Pleurotus ostreatus grain spawn with contaminated soil in two 7-gallon outdoor bins using a layering technique.

04

Growth Monitoring

Monitor fungal growth daily — recording temperature, humidity, and visual observations. Photograph bins at consistent angles.

05

Post-Experiment ICP-MS

After the growth phase, submit new soil aliquots from each bin to Caltech for a second round of lead concentration measurement.

06

Analysis & Comparison

Compare before/after ICP-MS data. Calculate percent change and consult Professor Leadbetter and Professor Tissot for interpretation.

Safety Considerations

All safety protocols were developed in consultation with Professor Jared Leadbetter (environmental microbiology, Caltech) and Professor François Tissot (geochemistry, Caltech).

  • Lead Soil Thresholds

    California screening level: 80 ppm (all normal activities permitted). EPA level: 200 ppm (wash hands after contact). Hazardous waste threshold: ~1,000 ppm. We are targeting soil in the 200–500 ppm range, confirmed with Professor Tissot.

  • Personal Protective Equipment

    N95 respirators during all soil handling, collection, and inoculation. Nitrile gloves and eye protection worn at all times when in contact with contaminated soil.

  • Experiment Location

    Per Professor Leadbetter's recommendation, the experiment is conducted outdoors in a non-moisture-controlled environment to prevent fungal pathogen buildup. Bins are kept out of direct sunlight.

  • Containment & Disposal

    Bins are covered with breathable fabric. Post-experiment mushrooms and soil will not be consumed and will be disposed of following appropriate protocols for lead-contaminated biological waste.

Key Finding — Test Strips Are Not Lead-Specific
To confirm our hypothesis that the strips were giving false positives, we tested Arjun's backyard — which is far from the fire area and unlikely to have elevated lead levels. It also came back positive at 400 ppm. This confirmed that the strips, despite being marketed as "Lead Soil Check," detect all heavy metals including iron, which is commonly found in soil. The results cannot be used to distinguish lead from other metals.
Decision
We concluded that relying on these strips to select the optimal soil would produce unreliable results. We decided to pivot to ICP-MS screening for all five samples, which will give us element-specific quantitative data. We emailed Professor Tissot the same day to explain the situation and ask for his guidance.

The Science
How mycoremediation works and why we chose oyster mushrooms

Pleurotus ostreatus were selected because they are among the most extensively studied species in mycoremediation research, with documented heavy metal bioaccumulation capacity. They are commercially available as grain spawn for consistent inoculation, relatively fast-growing, hardy in a range of outdoor conditions, and non-pathogenic with well-documented biology and handling guidelines.

Fungi can interact with heavy metals through several mechanisms: biosorption (passive binding of metals to cell wall components like chitin and glucans); bioaccumulation (active uptake of metals into the organism); and bioprecipitation (converting dissolved metals into insoluble forms). The fungal mycelium creates an enormous surface area that interacts with soil particles and pore water, making it an effective biological sorbent.

We use ICP-MS (Inductively Coupled Plasma Mass Spectrometry), provided through Professor Tissot's laboratory at Caltech. ICP-MS is capable of measuring elemental concentrations at parts-per-million (ppm) and even parts-per-billion sensitivity. Soil samples are analyzed before inoculation and after the experiment concludes; the comparison reveals any statistically significant changes in lead concentration.

The original MycoPod concept involved encapsulating oyster mushroom spawn within a hydrogel matrix for controlled deployment in arid soil. After consulting with Professor Leadbetter, we decided to remove the hydrogel component from this first iteration — it would require specialized machinery and expertise beyond what we currently have. This simplified proof-of-concept will validate fungal growth and lead sequestration first; the hydrogel system remains a planned enhancement for a future iteration.

The Project
Experimental choices, soil sourcing, and how this approach differs

Soil removal (excavation) is expensive, disruptive, and creates disposal challenges — contaminated soil removed from one site must be disposed of somewhere else, often in landfills or specialized facilities. Bioremediation approaches like mycoremediation offer a potentially more cost-effective, less disruptive in-situ alternative that treats contamination where it occurs rather than relocating it.

We explored several sources for lead-contaminated soil from the Eaton Fire affected area. Professor Tissot recommended a specific location in Altadena, and we also reached out to the USC CLEAN Project (Clean Los Angeles Earth Action Network), a research initiative focused on post-fire soil contamination. All soil acquisition prioritized safety for the researchers and compliance with appropriate environmental handling standards.

Future Directions
What happens after this experiment and whether this can scale

That is one of the most important open questions. This experiment is an intentionally small proof-of-concept to validate whether fungal sequestration of lead occurs under realistic field conditions. Scaling mycoremediation would require solving challenges around species selection for specific contaminants and climates, inoculation delivery mechanisms, monitoring at scale, and regulatory frameworks. A successful first iteration here would be one foundational data point in that larger picture.

If results show significant lead sequestration, we plan to share findings with Professors Leadbetter and Tissot and potentially publish them. A second iteration would reintroduce the hydrogel encapsulation system for more controlled deployment. We also envision expanding to test multiple fungal species, additional heavy metals (such as PAHs), and eventually designing a deployable system for use in fire-affected communities.
